Unsupervised Anomaly Detection in Pediatric Brain MRI via Latent Diffusion Models

Technical Briefing — Project Status & Case Study

1. Problem Statement & Motivation

1.1 Clinical Context

Pediatric brain tumour segmentation requires voxel-level annotation by expert neuroradiologists — a process that takes 15–30 minutes per volume and is subject to significant inter-rater variability (Menze et al., 2015). The BraTS challenge benchmarks supervised models that require hundreds of annotated volumes. We explore whether unsupervised anomaly detection (UAD) can bypass the annotation bottleneck entirely: a model trained exclusively on healthy brain tissue should detect tumours as deviations from the learned healthy manifold.

1.2 Hypothesis

Core Hypothesis
A Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) trained on healthy-only brain slices will learn a compressed representation of healthy anatomy. Given a test slice containing a tumour, partial noise injection followed by DDIM denoising will reconstruct a "healthy version" of that slice. The pixel-space residual $|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}|$ should localise the tumour.

2.4 Preprocessing Pipeline (Stage 0)

Each 3D volume is processed into 2D axial slices:

  1. Per-volume z-score normalisation over brain (non-zero) voxels:
$$x_{\text{norm}} = \frac{x - \mu_{\text{brain}}}{\sigma_{\text{brain}}}$$
  1. Robust percentile clipping at $[P_{0.5}, P_{99.5}]$ instead of a hard $\pm 3\sigma$ clip. This is critical: FLAIR hyperintensity from tumour edema routinely exceeds $3\sigma$; a hard clip would erase exactly the anomaly signal we need.
  2. Symmetric zero-padding to $256 \times 256$.
  3. Brain-fraction filter: Slices with $< 5\%$ non-zero voxels are discarded.
  4. Healthy buffer zone: A slice qualifies as "healthy" for diffusion training only if it is $\geq 3$ slices away from any lesion-bearing slice along the z-axis. This prevents mass-effect and edema contamination of the healthy training manifold.

3. Mathematical Foundations

3.1 Variational Autoencoder (VAE)

The KL-regularised autoencoder compresses the 4-channel MRI slice $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 256 \times 256}$ into a latent representation $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 32 \times 32}$ via a learned encoder $q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ and decoder $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$.

Encoder — Diagonal Gaussian Posterior

$$q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}\!\Big(\mathbf{z};\; \boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}),\; \text{diag}\!\big(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\phi(\mathbf{x})\big)\Big)$$

Reparameterisation Trick

Enables backpropagation through the stochastic bottleneck:

$$\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\sigma} \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$$

KL Divergence

Closed-form for diagonal Gaussians vs. standard normal prior:

$$D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \;\|\; \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\big) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left( \mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - 1 - \log \sigma_j^2 \right)$$

Training Objective

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \underbrace{\| \mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}} \|_1}_{\text{L1 Reconstruction}} \;+\; \lambda_{\text{SSIM}} \cdot \big(1 - \text{MS-SSIM}(\mathbf{x}, \hat{\mathbf{x}})\big) \;+\; \lambda_{\text{KL}} \cdot D_{\text{KL}}$$

where $\lambda_{\text{KL}} = 10^{-6}$ and $\lambda_{\text{SSIM}} = 0.5$.

Spatial Compression

The encoder applies 3 strided convolutions (factor $f = 2^3 = 8$), compressing $256 \times 256 \rightarrow 32 \times 32$. This is the critical design parameter that determines the information bottleneck.

3.2 Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM)

The diffusion model operates entirely in the VAE's latent space $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 32 \times 32}$.

Forward Process (Fixed)

Adds Gaussian noise according to a schedule $\{\beta_t\}_{t=1}^T$:

$$q(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\Big(\mathbf{z}_t;\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \mathbf{z}_0,\; (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\,\mathbf{I}\Big)$$

where $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$ and $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t} \alpha_s$.

Closed-form sampling (no iterative steps needed for the forward process):

$$\mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$$

Reverse Process (Learned)

The UNet $\epsilon_\theta$ is trained to predict the noise added at timestep $t$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t,\, \mathbf{z}_0,\, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \left[ \left\| \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t) \right\|^2 \right]$$

Noise Schedule: Cosine schedule (squaredcos_cap_v2) with $\beta_{\text{start}} = 0.0001$, $\beta_{\text{end}} = 0.02$, $T = 1000$.

3.3 DDIM Deterministic Sampling (Inference)

At inference, we use the deterministic DDIM update (Song et al., 2021) to skip timesteps:

$$\mathbf{z}_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{z}_t - \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \cdot \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}}_{\text{predicted } \mathbf{z}_0} \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \cdot \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t, t)$$

This allows denoising in $S = 50$ steps instead of $T = 1000$, reducing inference time by $20\times$.

3.4 Partial-Noise Reconstruction (The "Healing" Mechanism)

This is the core of the anomaly detection strategy, following Wyatt et al., 2022:

  1. Encode the test image: $\mathbf{z}_0 = \text{Enc}_\phi(\mathbf{x}_{\text{test}}) \cdot s_f$
  2. Add noise to an intermediate timestep $T_{\text{int}}$:
$$\mathbf{z}_{T_{\text{int}}} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{T_{\text{int}}}}\, \mathbf{z}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{T_{\text{int}}}}\, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$$
  1. Denoise from $T_{\text{int}} \rightarrow 0$ using the DDIM reverse steps. Since the UNet only knows healthy tissue, it "heals" the tumour while preserving healthy anatomy.
  2. Decode back to pixel space: $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \text{Dec}_\theta(\mathbf{z}_{\text{denoised}} / s_f)$

The intermediate timestep $T_{\text{int}} = 300$ (out of $T = 1000$) is a balance: too low preserves the tumour; too high destroys anatomical correspondence.

3.5 Latent Scaling Factor

The VAE's latent space has an empirically computed standard deviation $\sigma_z$. The scaling factor $s_f = 1/\sigma_z$ normalises latents to unit variance before the diffusion model operates on them. This is critical because the DDPM noise schedule assumes unit-variance inputs.

$$s_f = \frac{1}{\sigma_z} = \frac{1}{0.7927} = 1.2616$$

Computed over 2,000 randomly sampled slices; $\mu_z \approx 0$.

4.3 Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

An exponential moving average of the UNet weights is maintained during training with decay $\gamma = 0.999$:

$$\theta_{\text{EMA}}^{(t)} = \gamma \cdot \theta_{\text{EMA}}^{(t-1)} + (1 - \gamma) \cdot \theta^{(t)}$$

EMA weights produce markedly smoother, more stable samples and are used for all inference.

6. Anomaly Scoring Pipeline

6.1 Modality-Weighted Residual (Pixel Space)

Rather than a flat channel-mean residual, we weight MRI modalities by clinical salience:

$$S_{\text{pixel}}(\mathbf{x}, \hat{\mathbf{x}}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \cdot \text{clip}\!\Big(|\mathbf{x}_k - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k| - \mathbf{M}_{\text{baseline},\,k},\; 0\Big) \cdot \mathbf{B}$$

where:

  • $w_k$ are modality weights: $w_{\text{t1n}} = 0.5$, $w_{\text{t1c}} = 1.0$, $w_{\text{t2w}} = 0.75$, $w_{\text{t2f}} = 1.5$
  • $\mathbf{M}_{\text{baseline}}$ is the mean healthy residual (calibration baseline)
  • $\mathbf{B}$ is a binary brain mask (4-iteration erosion to exclude skull boundary)

Rationale for weights: FLAIR ($w = 1.5$) is the strongest edema marker; T1ce ($w = 1.0$) highlights enhancing tumour; T1-native ($w = 0.5$) adds the contrast-enhancement difference signal; T2w ($w = 0.75$) is moderate.

6.2 Contrast-Enhancement (CE) Channel

An additional synthetic channel captures the T1ce−T1n enhancement signal:

$$\text{CE}_{\text{orig}} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{t1c}} - \mathbf{x}_{\text{t1n}}, \qquad \text{CE}_{\text{recon}} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{t1c}} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\text{t1n}}$$
$$S_{\text{CE}} = |\text{CE}_{\text{orig}} - \text{CE}_{\text{recon}}|$$

This provides a direct measure of contrast enhancement change, which is the primary radiological marker for active/enhancing tumour tissue.

6.3 Multi-Timestep Aggregation

Instead of a single $T_{\text{int}} = 300$, the multi-T evaluation runs partial reconstruction at $T_{\text{int}} \in \{100, 250, 400\}$ and aggregates:

$$S_{\text{multi}} = \frac{1}{|T_{\text{list}}|} \sum_{T \in T_{\text{list}}} \frac{S_{\text{pixel}}^{(T)}}{\sigma_{\text{pixel}}^{(T)}}$$

Each $T$ captures different spatial scales: lower $T$ preserves fine detail, higher $T$ captures large structural deviations.

6.4 Calibration (Stage 3)

On a held-out set of $\leq 200$ healthy validation slices:

  1. Compute $\mathbf{M}_{\text{baseline}}(K, H, W)$ = mean residual stack over healthy tissue
  2. Compute pixel_scale = mean positive anomaly score on healthy tissue
  3. Set threshold = $P_{95}$ of all healthy brain voxel scores

This ensures the threshold is data-driven, not arbitrarily set.

7.2 Evaluation Protocol

  • Metrics are computed within the brain mask only (excluding background), as per standard BraTS evaluation.
  • AUROC is only computed for slices where both tumour and non-tumour voxels exist within the brain mask, and where the score has non-zero variance.
  • The brain mask is derived from the normalised image (any channel > $10^{-3}$), followed by 4-iteration binary erosion to exclude the skull boundary.

This ablation conclusively proves the necessity of the VAE compression for tractable generative modelling, while simultaneously revealing the VAE as the source of segmentation failure.

9. Failure Analysis: The VAE Bottleneck

9.1 Root Cause: High-Frequency Information Destruction

The $f = 8$ spatial compression ($256 \times 256 \rightarrow 32 \times 32$) acts as a spatial low-pass filter. High-frequency anatomical details — skull boundaries, cortical sulci, fine vascular structures — are irreversibly smoothed during encoding and cannot be recovered by the decoder.

9.2 The Halo Effect (Edge Artifact)

When computing the anomaly residual $|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}|$:

  • The sharp original image has crisp skull boundaries (pixel values change from 0 to ~1 over 1–2 pixels)
  • The blurry reconstruction has smooth, spread-out boundaries
  • The subtraction produces a ring of high residual around the entire brain perimeter

This "halo" has a higher magnitude than the actual tumour residual in many cases, which means:

  1. The calibration threshold (set at $P_{95}$ of healthy halo residuals) is pushed extremely high
  2. The actual tumour signal falls below this threshold
  3. The binary prediction mask misses the tumour → DICE ≈ 0

9.3 Why AUROC is High but DICE is Low

This is not contradictory:

  • AUROC measures ranking quality — can the model assign higher scores to tumour voxels than to non-tumour voxels? The answer is yes: within the brain interior, tumour voxels do tend to score higher.
  • DICE measures segmentation quality at a specific threshold. Because the skull halo contaminates the score distribution, no single threshold achieves clean segmentation.

9.4 The Fundamental Limitation of Reconstruction-Based UAD

This project reveals a deep structural limitation of reconstruction-based UAD for segmentation (as opposed to detection):

  • Detection (is there an anomaly in this image?) → AUROC > 0.90 ✓
  • Delineation (draw the exact pixel boundary of the anomaly) → DICE ~ 0.05 ✗

Reconstruction-based methods produce inherently diffuse, noisy anomaly maps because the residual captures all reconstruction errors (compression artifacts, stochastic noise, model imprecision), not just the anomaly. This is consistent with findings reported in Behrendt et al., 2024 and Bercea et al., 2023.

11. Future Work & Proposed Solutions

11.1 Conditional Diffusion Segmentation (MedSegDiff)

The most promising direction is to reformulate the task as supervised conditional diffusion segmentation (Wu et al., "MedSegDiff", MICCAI 2023):

  • The UNet is conditioned on the input MRI (concatenated as additional channels)
  • It learns to denoise a segmentation mask rather than the image itself
  • Achieves DICE 0.75–0.85 on BraTS while still using diffusion models

11.2 VAE Architecture Improvements

If remaining within the UAD framework:

  • Perceptual loss (LPIPS) + adversarial patch discriminator → sharper reconstructions
  • Reduce compression factor from $f = 8$ to $f = 4$ ($64 \times 64$ latents)
  • Increase latent channels from 4 to 8–16 → wider information bottleneck

11.3 Simplex Noise (AnoDDPM)

Replace Gaussian noise with spatially-correlated simplex noise during the partial-noise step. This preserves coarse anatomical structure while still corrupting fine tumour detail, reducing false positives at edges.

12. Conclusion

This project demonstrates a successful implementation of a complete Latent Diffusion Model pipeline for unsupervised anomaly detection in pediatric brain MRI. The pipeline achieves:

  • ✅ Strong anomaly discrimination (AUROC = 0.90) — the model successfully identifies the presence and approximate location of tumours
  • ✅ Complete end-to-end system — from NIfTI preprocessing through VAE training, diffusion training, calibration, and quantitative evaluation
  • ✅ Rigorous evaluation — patient-level splits, brain-masked metrics, multi-threshold analysis, oracle ceiling computation

The case study reveals a fundamental bottleneck in the VAE's spatial compression that prevents translation from detection performance to segmentation performance — a finding consistent with recent literature (Behrendt et al., 2024). The pixel-space ablation study provides conclusive evidence that naive removal of the VAE is not a viable solution, confirming that the problem requires an architectural pivot (e.g., conditional diffusion segmentation) rather than a parameter-level fix.
