\section*{Abstract}
We present a \textbf{pixel-space patch-diffusion model (PDM)} for \textbf{unsupervised
anomaly detection (UAD)} in pediatric brain MRI, trained on the BraTS-PEDs cohort
with \textbf{no lesion labels}. A four-channel denoising diffusion model
(89.5\,M-parameter UNet) learns the distribution of \emph{healthy} $96{\times}96$
image patches under spatially-correlated (simplex) noise; at inference a test slice
is partially noised and DDIM-denoised back onto the healthy manifold, and the
multi-scale, modality-weighted reconstruction residual localises pathology. The
healthy reconstruction is itself a faithful \emph{counterfactual}, complemented by
exact per-modality / per-scale residual attribution. On a
30-patient pilot (60 epochs, NVIDIA L4) the diffusion model reaches a best
validation noise-MSE of \textbf{0.0604} ($\approx$94\% of injected-noise variance
explained). Over 204 evaluated test slices the system attains
\textbf{AUROC $=0.713$}, \textbf{AUPRC $=0.076$}, calibrated \textbf{Dice $=0.076$}
(oracle $0.138$); on a well-represented patient it reaches AUROC $0.96$ and slice
Dice up to \textbf{0.58}, competitive with published adult-BraTS UAD. A rigorous,
leakage-controlled failure analysis identifies the exact bottleneck: the score is
governed by \emph{per-patient reconstruction fidelity}, so atypical (out-of-manifold)
anatomy produces a uniformly high residual in which the lesion is not separable
(AUROC $<0.5$), whereas the operating threshold is shown to be near-optimal and is
\emph{not} the limiting factor. We conclude the approach is sound and the dominant
lever is healthy-manifold coverage (training scale), not the detector design.
\end{minipage}\end{center}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
Detecting tumours in brain MRI is classically posed as \emph{supervised}
segmentation, which requires large sets of expert voxel annotations and, by
construction, only learns the pathologies present in the training labels.
\textbf{Unsupervised anomaly detection (UAD)} inverts this: a generative model
learns the distribution of \emph{healthy} anatomy, and any sufficiently large
deviation is flagged as anomalous \cite{baur2021,schlegl2019}. This removes the
annotation bottleneck and is, in principle, agnostic to the lesion type --- it
responds to \emph{any} departure from normality --- which is especially valuable
in the \textbf{pediatric} setting, where tumours are heterogeneous and labelled
data are scarce \cite{kazerooni2024}.

Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) \cite{ho2020} have become the
generative backbone of choice for reconstruction-based UAD \cite{wyatt2022,
behrendt2023}: a model trained only on healthy data, asked to denoise a corrupted
pathological image, tends to ``in-paint'' the abnormal region with the most
probable healthy continuation, so the lesion survives as a reconstruction residual.
This work designs, trains and critically evaluates such a system for pediatric
brain MRI, and --- crucially --- delivers an \textbf{explainable, leakage-controlled
diagnosis of when and why it succeeds or fails}.

\subsection{Problem statement and objectives}
Given an unseen multi-modal MRI slice, produce a per-pixel anomaly map (and a
binary lesion prediction) using a model trained \emph{only} on lesion-free anatomy.
Objectives: (a) learn a healthy-manifold diffusion model over multi-modal MRI
patches; (b) convert reconstruction residuals into a calibrated, label-free anomaly
score; (c) explain inference behaviour with faithful, decision-intrinsic
explainers (counterfactual generation and exact residual attribution); (d) quantify
performance with AUROC/AUPRC/Dice and compare
against the literature; and (e) identify the exact failure mechanism with
figure-backed, leakage-proof evidence.

% =====================================================================
\section{Related Work}
% =====================================================================
\textbf{Reconstruction-based UAD.} Convolutional and variational autoencoders score
$\|\x-g(\x)\|$ against a learned healthy model \cite{baur2021}; f-AnoGAN replaces the
decoder with a GAN generator plus a discriminator-feature residual \cite{schlegl2019}.
Their recurring weakness is the \emph{identity shortcut}: a model expressive enough
to reconstruct healthy tissue often reconstructs the lesion too, collapsing the
residual.

\textbf{Diffusion UAD.} AnoDDPM \cite{wyatt2022} introduced \emph{partial}-noise
reconstruction and \textbf{simplex (correlated) noise}, which attacks the
mid-/large spatial scale of lesions rather than pixel detail. pDDPM
\cite{behrendt2023} reconstructs in overlapping \emph{patches} to improve
healthy-tissue fidelity. Multi-noise-level scoring \cite{bercea2023,graham2023}
adapts sensitivity to lesion size, and weakly-/latent-guided variants push Dice
further \cite{wolleb2022,pinaya2022}. Reporting Dice at calibrated, best-global and
oracle thresholds is standard because the gap quantifies thresholding headroom
\cite{zimmerer2022,baur2021}.

\textbf{Our position.} We combine partial-noise DDIM reconstruction, simplex noise,
patch-wise diffusion with Gaussian fusion, multi-scale scoring, and a
modality-weighted contrast-enhancement residual into a single pipeline, and add a
\emph{counterfactual-first} explainability layer. The novelty for this project is
less any single component than the \textbf{rigorous, leakage-controlled diagnostic
evaluation} (\S\ref{sec:failure}).

% =====================================================================
\section{Approach and Justification}
% =====================================================================
Each design choice is evidence-based:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Pixel-space (not latent) diffusion} --- avoids the VAE bottleneck that,
in our predecessor latent pipeline, dominated the residual with edge artefacts; the
residual is computed directly in interpretable image space.
\item \textbf{Patch diffusion} \cite{behrendt2023} --- turns $\sim$3k healthy slices
into $\sim$570k $96{\times}96$ training patches ($\sim$196$\times$ data
amplification), decisive in the low-patient pediatric regime, and forces a
\emph{local} texture model that cannot memorise whole-slice layout.
\item \textbf{Simplex noise} \cite{wyatt2022} --- low-frequency-dominated corruption
matched to blob-like tumours (\S\ref{sec:method}, Fig.~\ref{fig:schedule}).
\item \textbf{Multi-scale scoring} \cite{bercea2023} --- $T\in\{50,150,250\}$ covers
fine (high-SNR) to structural (low-SNR) anomalies.
\item \textbf{Modality weighting + CE channel} \cite{menze2015} --- FLAIR (edema) and
contrast-enhancement ($t1c-t1n$) dominate tumour signal.
\item \textbf{Counterfactual-first XAI} \cite{wachter2017,sanchez2022,wolleb2022} ---
the healthy reconstruction \emph{is} the decision variable, so the explanation is
faithful by construction; post-hoc saliency (Grad-CAM) is \emph{rejected} as
unfaithful for a generative denoiser \cite{adebayo2018,rudin2019} (\S\ref{sec:xai}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Data, MRI Modalities and Preprocessing}
% =====================================================================
\subsection{Dataset and the four MRI modalities}
We use \textbf{BraTS-PEDs} \cite{kazerooni2024}, each patient providing four
co-registered, skull-stripped MRI modalities and an expert tumour segmentation used
\emph{only for evaluation}. The modalities are physically complementary:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{T1 native ($t1n$)} --- anatomical reference; fluids dark, fat/white-matter bright.
\item \textbf{T1 contrast-enhanced ($t1c$)} --- gadolinium highlights blood-brain-barrier
breakdown, marking \emph{active} tumour.
\item \textbf{T2-weighted ($t2w$)} --- fluids bright; reveals edema and cystic components.
\item \textbf{T2-FLAIR ($t2f$)} --- T2 with CSF suppressed; the most sensitive to
peritumoural edema and infiltration.
\end{itemize}
No single modality captures a tumour: enhancement is a $t1c$ phenomenon, edema a
$t2f/t2w$ one. We therefore retain all four and add an explicit
\textbf{contrast-enhancement (CE) residual channel} $t1c-t1n$ \cite{menze2015}.

\paragraph{How one model processes all four modalities jointly.}
The four modalities are stacked into a single global \textbf{4-channel slice
tensor} $\x_{\text{slice}}\in\R^{4\times256\times256}$, from which the
$96{\times}96$ training/inference patches $\x\in\R^{4\times96\times96}$ are
subsequently extracted (\S\ref{sec:method}). The diffusion UNet operates on these
4-channel \emph{patches} with \texttt{in\_channels}=\texttt{out\_channels}=4, so the
very first convolution mixes the four modalities at every spatial location and
\emph{all} subsequent features are jointly modality-aware; the network predicts a
4-channel noise tensor $\eps_\theta$ in one forward pass. The four modalities are thus denoised \emph{coherently} (a structure
must be explained consistently across modalities to be reconstructed), and the
\emph{score} re-weights the per-channel residuals by clinical salience plus the CE
channel (\S\ref{sec:method}). This is strictly richer than per-modality models,
which cannot exploit cross-modal consistency.

\subsection{Preprocessing and its justification}
(i) \textbf{Axial slicing} to $256{\times}256$. (ii) \textbf{Robust intensity
normalisation} per modality to $[-1,1]$ using the $[0.5,99.5]$ percentile clip
rather than $\pm3\sigma$ --- the latter truncates exactly the FLAIR/enhancement
hyper-intensities that carry the lesion signal. (iii) \textbf{Healthy-pool
construction}: a slice enters diffusion training only if lesion-free \emph{and}
$\ge3$ slices from any lesion, preventing partial-volume tumour leakage into the
``healthy'' manifold. (iv) \textbf{Patch extraction}: overlapping $96{\times}96$
patches, stride $16$, foreground fraction $\ge0.10$.

\subsection{Data-leakage prevention (why the numbers are honest)}
\label{sec:leakage}
Every result in this report is protected against optimistic bias by four explicit
controls, so the reported metrics neither over- nor under-state the method:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Patient-level disjoint splits.} Train/validation/test are partitioned
by \emph{patient ID}; no slice of a test patient is ever seen in training or
calibration. This rules out the most common medical-imaging leak (adjacent slices
of one patient straddling the split).
\item \textbf{Label-free training.} The diffusion model and the VAE-free residual
use \emph{no} segmentation masks; masks appear only in the final metric computation.
There is therefore no path for label information to enter the model.
\item \textbf{Healthy-only manifold + buffer.} The $\ge3$-slice lesion buffer
prevents tumour-adjacent tissue from contaminating the healthy distribution, so the
model cannot ``learn the lesion'' and then trivially reconstruct it.
\item \textbf{Calibration on held-out healthy slices.} The operating threshold
$\tau$ is fit on healthy validation slices only (\S\ref{sec:method}); it never sees
test data or any lesion.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently the strong result on patient P00002 is \emph{not} leakage, and the
weak result on P00216 is \emph{not} an artefact of an unfair split --- both are
genuine generalisation behaviour on unseen patients (\S\ref{sec:failure}).

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% =====================================================================
We summarise the formulation here; the complete step-by-step derivation (forward
kernel, ELBO, posterior, $\eps$-objective, DDIM, scoring, calibration, metrics) is
in Appendix~\ref{app:math}.

\textbf{Forward/where-it-trains.} For a clean patch $\x_0$ (healthy), the diffusion
kernel gives, with $\abar_t=\prod_{s\le t}(1-\beta_s)$,
\begin{equation}
\x_t=\sqrt{\abar_t}\,\x_0+\sqrt{1-\abar_t}\,\bm n,
\end{equation}
where $\bm n$ is unit-variance \emph{simplex} noise. The UNet $\eps_\theta$ is
trained by the noise-prediction objective
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}}=\E_{\x_0,\,t,\,\bm n}\big\|\bm n-\eps_\theta(\x_t,t)\big\|_2^2,
\end{equation}
over healthy patches only, with the cosine schedule
($T{=}1000$, $\beta\in[10^{-4},2{\times}10^{-2}]$).

\textbf{Inference (partial-noise counterfactual).} A test patch is noised only to an
intermediate $T$, then DDIM-denoised ($\eta{=}0$) to $\hat\x_0^{\text{healthy}}$; the
predicted clean patch is
\begin{equation}
\hat\x_0(\x_t,t)=\frac{\x_t-\sqrt{1-\abar_t}\,\eps_\theta(\x_t,t)}{\sqrt{\abar_t}} .
\end{equation}
Healthy tissue is reconstructed faithfully; localised pathology is ``healed,'' so
the residual localises it.

\textbf{Multi-scale, modality-weighted score.} Per-modality residuals
$r_k=|\x_0^{(k)}-\hat\x_0^{(k)}|$ plus the CE channel are combined with weights
$\bm w\propto(0.5,1.0,0.75,1.5)$ ($w_{\text{CE}}{=}1$) and fused across overlapping
patches by a Gaussian window; per-noise-scale maps at $T\in\{50,150,250\}$ are
combined with weights $(0.25,0.45,0.30)$:
\begin{equation}
S(\bm u)=\Big(\textstyle\sum_{T}\omega_T\,S_T(\bm u)\Big)\,m_{\text{brain}}(\bm u).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Calibration.} The threshold is the 95th percentile of pooled \emph{healthy}
brain-voxel scores, giving a controlled label-free false-positive rate; here
$\tau=0.128$.

% =====================================================================
\section{Training and Strategy}
% =====================================================================
The model was trained on a single \textbf{NVIDIA L4 (24\,GB)} with bf16 AMP, EMA
(decay $0.999$), gradient clipping, warmup$\to$cosine LR (peak $2{\times}10^{-4}$),
and early stopping on the healthy-patch validation loss. To fit the compute budget
the per-epoch patch count was capped at 250k (a random subset, reshuffled each
epoch), so one epoch $\approx$977 steps $\approx$9.4\,min; the full run was
\textbf{60 epochs ($\approx$9.4\,h)}. Key hyper-parameters: patch $96$, stride $16$;
UNet block widths $(128,256,384,512)$, \textbf{89.5\,M parameters}; simplex noise
(6 octaves, persistence $0.8$, lacunarity $2.0$).

\subsection{Training and validation behaviour}
The validation noise-MSE (Fig.~\ref{fig:training}) confirms a well-trained,
non-overfit denoiser: it decreases monotonically with the LR cycles and plateaus at
$0.0604$. This rules out under-training or over-fitting as explanations for the
downstream failures (\S\ref{sec:failure}).

% =====================================================================
\section{The Exact Problem: Leakage-Controlled Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}
% =====================================================================
We now isolate, with figure-backed evidence, exactly why the calibrated Dice is low,
having already excluded data leakage (\S\ref{sec:leakage}), under-training and
over-fitting (Fig.~\ref{fig:training}).

\subsection{It is a score-separability problem, not a threshold problem}
Fig.~\ref{fig:hist} contrasts the per-voxel score distributions. For the success
slice (P00002 $z094$) the \emph{healthy} voxels peak at low scores ($\sim$0.05) and
the \emph{lesion} voxels peak high ($\sim$0.15): the two are well separated and the
calibrated $\tau=0.128$ sits cleanly between them, giving Dice $0.58$. For the
failure slice (P00216 $z037$) the healthy and lesion distributions are
\textbf{almost completely overlapping} --- the score simply does not distinguish
lesion from healthy tissue (slice AUROC $0.44$, below chance).

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\begin{subfigure}{0.49\textwidth}\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{r-hist-success.png}\caption{Success (P00002\_z094)}\end{subfigure}\hfill
\begin{subfigure}{0.49\textwidth}\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{r-hist-failure.png}\caption{Failure (P00216\_z037)}\end{subfigure}
\caption{Healthy vs.\ lesion score distributions (log-density), with $\tau=0.128$.
Clean separation (left) vs.\ near-total overlap (right). The failure is an inability
of the \emph{score} to separate the classes.}
\label{fig:hist}
\end{figure}

Crucially, where detection works, the threshold itself is \textbf{not} the
bottleneck. Fig.~\ref{fig:thr} sweeps the threshold over the \emph{four diagnostic
slices} examined in this section (the two success and two failure cases above);
because this small sample is success-weighted, its absolute Dice ($\approx0.28$) is
far higher than the 204-slice corpus mean ($0.076$) and should not be read as a
corpus figure. The informative quantity is the curve's \emph{shape}: the calibrated
$\tau=0.128$ (mean Dice $0.284$) lies within $0.005$ Dice of the best global
threshold $\tau^\star=0.135$ (mean Dice $0.289$), i.e.\ the operating point is
near-optimal on slices where the score separates the classes. Corpus-wide, the
calibrated-to-oracle gap ($0.076\!\to\!0.138$, Table~\ref{tab:results}) shows a
single global threshold still leaves $\approx0.06$ Dice on the table across
heterogeneous slices --- a secondary lever --- but the dominant failure (P00216,
slice AUROC $<0.5$) is \emph{threshold-independent}: no threshold recovers a score
that does not rank lesion above healthy tissue.

\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{r-dice-threshold.png}

\subsection{Root cause: per-patient reconstruction fidelity / out-of-manifold anatomy}
Why can the score separate lesions on P00002 but not P00216?
Fig.~\ref{fig:maskedge} shows that on the failure patient the anomaly score is high
across the \emph{entire} brain parenchyma --- not the lesion --- and
Fig.~\ref{fig:modres} shows the per-modality residual is diffuse across all
modalities, not localised. The model reconstructs this patient's \emph{healthy}
tissue poorly, so every voxel has a large residual and the lesion contrast vanishes.
In other words, P00216's anatomy is \textbf{out-of-distribution} for the healthy
manifold learned from only 30 training patients; the residual then measures
``distance from the training manifold,'' which is uniformly large, rather than
``local pathology''. P00002, whose anatomy is well represented and whose tumour is a
compact contrast-enhancing mass, yields the intended localised residual.

\subsection{Two distinct, separable failure modes}
The per-slice scatter (Fig.~\ref{fig:da}) makes the taxonomy explicit:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Detection failure} (P00216): slice AUROC $\le0.5$. The score ranks
lesion voxels no better than (sometimes worse than) healthy ones. \emph{No}
threshold recovers it (oracle Dice also low, $0.073$). Cause: OOD anatomy / poor
healthy reconstruction.
\item \textbf{Precision / small-lesion failure} (P00076, parts of P00203): AUROC is
respectable ($0.65$--$0.78$) but Dice and AUPRC are low because the lesion is small
relative to a diffuse low-level false-positive field, so precision collapses.
\end{enumerate}
Both are downstream of the same lever --- the fidelity and coverage of the healthy
manifold --- which a 30-patient pilot under-provides.

\subsection{What is \emph{not} the cause (controls)}
For completeness we state the eliminated hypotheses, each with its evidence:
data leakage (\S\ref{sec:leakage}, patient-level splits + label-free training);
under-training / over-fitting (Fig.~\ref{fig:training}, smooth converged val loss);
threshold miscalibration (Fig.~\ref{fig:thr}, $\tau$ within $0.005$ Dice of
optimal); and scoring-weight error (Fig.~\ref{fig:modres}, residual is diffuse in
\emph{every} modality, so re-weighting cannot localise it).

% =====================================================================
\section{Explainability (XAI) and Inference Behaviour}
\label{sec:xai}
% =====================================================================
\subsection{Why post-hoc saliency is the wrong tool here}
A natural first instinct is to apply Grad-CAM \cite{selvaraju2017} for a ``where did
the model look'' map. This is \emph{unfaithful} for a generative diffusion UAD model:
Grad-CAM localises the regions that raise a single \emph{class logit}, but a
denoiser has no class head. The only available scalar is the dense-regression noise
error, whose global-average-pooled gradient collapses all spatial information into a
per-channel weight, leaving the map with no spatial selectivity. Empirically it is
diffuse \emph{even on slices where the anomaly score sharply localises the lesion}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:xai}, top-right was such a failed map) --- failing the sanity
criterion that an explanation track the model's output \cite{adebayo2018} and the
faithfulness requirement for high-stakes use \cite{rudin2019}. We therefore reject
post-hoc CAM and use explainers \emph{intrinsic} to the decision.

\subsection{Faithful explainers for this pipeline}
\textbf{(i) Counterfactual generation (primary).} The healthy reconstruction
$\hat\x$ \emph{is} the thresholded decision variable, so $|\x-\hat\x|$ is faithful by
construction (no surrogate), and $\hat\x$ is the projection of the patient onto the
healthy manifold/prototype (Appendix~\ref{app:math}, Eq.~\eqref{eq:prototype})
\cite{wachter2017,sanchez2022,wolleb2022}. Fig.~\ref{fig:xai} shows it isolates a
sharp tumour hot-spot on the success slice and is diffuse on the failure slice ---
\emph{visually predicting the metric outcome without the ground truth}.
\textbf{(ii) Residual attribution (supporting).} Because the score is an
\emph{exact additive} sum over modalities and scales, attribution is exact:
Fig.~\ref{fig:attr} decomposes the score by modality, showing FLAIR ($t2f$)
dominates the genuine lesion (success) while the failure case spreads more evenly
across modalities --- the signature of a whole-brain reconstruction error rather
than localised pathology. \textbf{(iii) Per-scale / partial-diffusion}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:attr}b) shows each noise level's contribution; for compact tumours
the higher-SNR scales localise best. Together these turn each prediction into a
checkable statement and \emph{flag the failures} (diffuse counterfactual + even
modality split) label-free.

\textbf{Is it a good idea?} Yes, with a qualifier. On its best slice (P00002\,$z094$)

by \emph{out-of-manifold patients} under a 30-patient training budget --- a
data-coverage limitation, not a flaw in the detector. The headroom is therefore in
training scale and reconstruction fidelity, both directly addressable
(\S\ref{sec:future}).

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
% =====================================================================
\textbf{Limitations:} 30-patient pilot (limited manifold coverage); evaluation on a
204-slice subset (full 1000-slice run was compute-prohibitive at $\sim$101\,s/slice);
inter-patient variance; residual partly affected by the brain boundary.
\textbf{Future work:} (1) train on the full $\sim$180-patient cohort to broaden the
healthy manifold (the single highest-leverage fix for the detection failures);
(2) add an OOD/quality gate that flags low-fidelity reconstructions (high global
residual) before scoring, converting silent failures into abstentions; (3) per-slice
or learned adaptive thresholding to close the calibrated$\to$oracle gap on the
precision-limited patients; (4) boundary-aware masking; (5) full-cohort evaluation
with the best-global and oracle operating points.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =====================================================================
We built and rigorously evaluated a patch-diffusion UAD pipeline for pediatric brain
MRI, with a faithful explainability layer and a leakage-controlled analysis. The
diffusion model trains cleanly (val noise-MSE $0.0604$) and, on well-represented
anatomy, localises tumours competitively (Dice up to $0.58$, AUROC $0.96$). The
\emph{exact} pilot bottleneck is precisely identified and substantiated: the anomaly
score is governed by per-patient reconstruction fidelity, so out-of-manifold
patients yield a uniformly high residual in which the lesion is not separable, while
the operating threshold is shown to be near-optimal. The remedy --- broader
healthy-manifold coverage via full-cohort training --- is concrete and falsifiable,
making this a sound foundation rather than a dead end.
